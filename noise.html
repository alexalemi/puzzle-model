Noise Structure Explorer

Empirical analysis of residual noise in the speed puzzling model

Model: Joint Solo + Team (2r) Model Explorer Tournament Predictions

Overview

The model predicts solving time as log(time) = μ + αi + βj + f(N) + ε, where α is puzzler ability, β is puzzle difficulty, f(N) is a piece-count correction, and ε is residual noise. This page investigates the empirical structure of ε — how it's distributed, whether it's truly independent across competitors, and what fraction is stable person-specific bias vs genuine randomness.

Understanding noise structure matters for tournament simulation: if noise is correlated within events or persistent across rounds, naive independent sampling produces incorrect win probabilities.

2. Residual Distribution

The model assumes Student-t noise with ν ≈ 3 degrees of freedom, giving heavier tails than a Normal distribution. How well does this match the empirical residuals?

Residuals by data source

Splitting by source reveals whether the bimodal shape comes from mixing distinct populations. All residuals are standardized with the global mean and std so the histograms are directly comparable.

Residuals by puzzle observation count

If the bimodality comes from beta shrinkage, puzzles with few observations should show broader, more asymmetric residuals (the model's prior pulls their difficulty toward zero). Well-observed puzzles should have tight, unimodal residuals.

Residuals by division

Splitting by division (solo / duo / group) tests whether team solving introduces a distinct noise regime. The model fits individual α parameters — for team entries these absorb the team identity, but the noise structure may differ.

Residuals by puzzler observation count

Analogous to the puzzle-count split above, but now grouping by how many times each puzzler appears. If poorly- measured puzzlers (few observations) have wider residuals, that's α estimation noise. If it persists for well- measured puzzlers, the model's single global σ is too simple.

Residuals by skill × difficulty quadrant

Does the additive model break down for certain combinations of puzzler skill and puzzle difficulty? Splitting at the median α (puzzler) and β (puzzle) creates four groups. If the model is truly additive, all four should have similar residual distributions — systematic differences indicate an interaction effect the model misses.

3. Within-Event Noise Correlation

When multiple competitors solve the same puzzle at the same event, are their residuals correlated? If so, there's a shared environmental component (venue conditions, timing, etc.) beyond the puzzle difficulty β.

Method: group observations by (event, puzzle). Within each group, compute residuals, subtract the group mean, and measure pairwise correlation of deviations.
